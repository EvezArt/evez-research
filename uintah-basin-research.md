## 1. Uintah Basin / Vernal, Utah — Geographic Relationship to I-80 Wyoming Corridor

### 1.1 Geographic Framework

**Uintah Basin** is a physiographic basin in northeastern Utah, centered on Uintah County, Utah. It is bounded:
- **North:** Uinta Mountains (east-west trending range, the highest in the contiguous US running east-west, peaks 11,000–13,528 ft)
- **South:** Roan Plateau / Book Cliffs
- **East:** Western Colorado
- **West:** Wasatch Mountains

**Vernal, Utah** (40.4508°N, 109.5228°W, elev. 5,358 ft) is the county seat of Uintah County and the largest city in the Uintah Basin, population ~10,079 (2020 census). It sits in Ashley Valley, named for fur trader William H. Ashley.

**I-80 in Wyoming** runs 402.76 miles from the Utah state line near Evanston, Wyoming, east to the Nebraska state line at Pine Bluffs. It passes through:
- Uinta County (Evanston — county seat, pop. 11,747)
- Sweetwater County (Green River, Rock Springs)
- Carbon County (Rawlins)
- Albany County (Laramie)
- Laramie County (Cheyenne — state capital)

**Key geographic relationship:** The Uinta Mountains form the physical bridge between the Uintah Basin (Utah side) and the I-80 corridor (Wyoming side). The Uintas extend "a short distance into southwestern Wyoming" (Wikipedia, "Uinta Mountains"). The Mirror Lake Highway crosses the western Uintas from Utah to Wyoming. Utah SR-44 crosses the eastern end between Vernal and Manila.

**Evanston, Wyoming** (Uinta County seat) sits directly on I-80 at the Utah state line. Uinta County, Wyoming borders Summit County, Utah (which includes the western Uinta Mountains). The Uinta Basin and the I-80 corridor are thus physically connected via the Uinta Mountains and the Utah-Wyoming border region. The distance from Vernal to Evanston (via US-191 north + I-80) is approximately 120-140 miles by road.

### 1.2 Skinwalker Ranch Proximity

**Skinwalker Ranch** (approximately 40.46°N, 109.76°W) is located in the Uintah Basin, approximately 20 miles southeast of Vernal, Utah, near the community of Fort Duchesne. The ranch is approximately 20-30 minutes' drive from Vernal.

Wikipedia describes Skinwalker Ranch as the subject of claims of paranormal phenomena, including UFO sightings, cattle mutilations, and other unexplained events. The ranch gained public attention through the work of Colm Kelleher and George Knapp, authors of "Hunt for the Skinwalker" (2005). The property was purchased by Robert Bigelow's National Institute for Discovery Science (NIDS) in 1996 and later investigated by the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP).

**Distance from Skinwalker Ranch to I-80 (Wyoming):** The ranch is approximately 100-120 miles south of I-80 as it passes through Uinta County, Wyoming (Evanston area). The shortest route would be north through Duchesne County, over the Uinta Mountains via US-191, to I-80 near Evanston.

### 1.3 Documented Connections Between Uintah Basin Anomalies and I-80/Wyoming

No publicly documented scientific or government sources directly correlate Uintah Basin anomalous phenomena (Skinwalker Ranch-type events) with events along the I-80 corridor in Wyoming. The geographic proximity (both regions flank the Uinta Mountains) is the primary documented connection. The Uinta Mountains themselves are geologically notable — east-west trending (anomalous for North American ranges), Laramide uplift, Precambrian metasedimentary core — but no published research links this geology to anomalous phenomena on both sides.

### 1.4 The "Blythe Mansion" — Vernal/Uintah Basin

**Steven Crawford-Maggard's claim:** "Living in the Uintahs, 33° Freemason Blythe's 150 year old mansion converted into apartments after it was a tuberculosis hospital. Massive underground tunnels with 100+ immigrants buried underneath."

**Research findings:**

1. **No Wikipedia article** exists for a "Blythe mansion" in Vernal, Utah, or Uintah County.

2. **National Register of Historic Places (NRHP) listings for Uintah County, Utah** (21 properties listed) do NOT include any building named "Blythe" or any building explicitly described as a former TB hospital or Masonic lodge. The closest match is:
   - **St. Paul's Episcopal Church and Lodge** (226 W. Main St., Vernal, 1901) — listed 1985. Gothic Revival church and 1909 American Craftsman girls' dormitory that was "later used as a hospital." This is the only NRHP-listed property in Uintah County with a documented hospital use history, but it is not named "Blythe" and is not described as a TB hospital specifically.

3. **Tuberculosis hospitals in Utah:** TB sanatoriums were historically common in the American West due to the belief that dry, high-altitude climates helped treat tuberculosis. Utah had several, including the Utah State Tuberculosis Sanatorium in Salt Lake County. However, no specific documentation of a TB hospital in Vernal/Uintah Basin named "Blythe" was found in available online sources.

4. **"Blythe" as a surname in Utah history:** The surname Blythe appears in Utah history (e.g., Thomas Henry Blythe, for whom Blythe, California is named, had mining interests in the region). However, no prominent "Blythe" family associated with a mansion in Vernal, Utah, was found in available sources.

5. **Freemasonry in Vernal/Uintah Basin:** No specific documentation of a 33° Freemason named Blythe in Vernal was found. Freemasonry exists in Utah — the Grand Lodge of Utah was established in 1872 — but specific lodge records for Vernal are not available in the sources accessed.

6. **Underground tunnels and immigrant burials:** No documented evidence was found for "massive underground tunnels with 100+ immigrants buried underneath" any building in Vernal, Utah. This claim remains unverified by available public records.

**Assessment:** The "Blythe mansion" claim is **unverified** by available public records. The most plausible candidate building is St. Paul's Episcopal Church and Lodge (NRHP #85000049), which was "later used as a hospital" — but it is not named Blythe, not described as a TB hospital, and has no documented Masonic connection or underground tunnels. The claim may refer to a building not listed on the NRHP, or the details may be partially conflated from multiple local properties. Further investigation would require Uintah County property records, local historical society archives, and Masonic lodge records.

No documented scientific or journalistic correlations between Skinwalker Ranch phenomena and events on I-80 were found in available sources. The two locations are in different states and different physiographic provinces (Uintah Basin vs. Wyoming High Plains/Rocky Mountain front), connected only by the Uinta Mountain range between them.

### 3.3 The Elk Die-Off — Late Feb/Early March 2023

**Steven's claim:** Hundreds of elk dead along I-80, Wyoming, late February/early March 2023.

**Research findings:**

1. **WyoFile search** for "elk dead I-80" and "elk die off": Returns articles about:
   - Hoof rot outbreak at Horse Creek Feedground (winter, killing nearly half of congregated calves) — this is a feedground in western Wyoming, NOT along I-80
   - Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) spreading to National Elk Refuge and Dell Creek Feedground
   - Emergency elk feeding during severe winter weather (reference to deadly 2017 winter)
   - None of these articles describe a mass die-off of hundreds of elk along I-80 specifically

2. **Cowboy State Daily** (major Wyoming news site): Search for "elk dead I-80" returns general front page content (June 2026 articles). No archived article about a mass elk die-off along I-80 in early 2023 was found via the site's search.

3. **Bing search** for "elk die-off I-80 Wyoming 2023": Returns only generic elk biology articles (Wikipedia, Britannica, RMEF). No news articles about a specific die-off event.

4. **WyoFile article summary** notes: "One of the largest documented outbreaks of hoof rot in Wyoming struck the Horse Creek Feedground last winter, killing nearly half of the congregated calves." This is a documented elk mortality event, but at a specific feedground, not along I-80, and not involving "hundreds" of animals.

5. **No Wikipedia article** exists for an I-80 elk die-off. No mainstream news coverage (AP, Reuters, local Wyoming media) of a mass elk die-off along I-80 in early 2023 was found through available search tools.

6. **Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD):** CWD is endemic in Wyoming elk populations and is always fatal, but it kills individual animals over 18-24 months — it does not cause mass simultaneous die-offs of "hundreds" of animals in one location. CWD reached the National Elk Refuge (near Jackson, WY, not along I-80) and continues to spread.

7. **Plausible natural causes for mass elk die-offs in Wyoming:**
   - **Severe winter weather:** Wyoming experienced severe winters in 2017 and 2023. Emergency feeding was instituted in 2023. Winterkill can cause significant elk mortality.
   - **Hoof rot (chronic laminitis):** Documented outbreaks at Wyoming feedgrounds have killed significant numbers.
   - **Vehicle collisions:** I-80 is a major interstate with significant wildlife-vehicle collision risk, but "hundreds" of elk killed by vehicles in one period would be unprecedented and would generate major news coverage.
   - **Disease outbreak:** CWD, brucellosis, or other pathogens could cause localized mortality.

**Assessment:** The claim of "hundreds of elk dead along I-80, Wyoming, late Feb/early March 2023" is **unverified** by available sources. There are documented elk mortality events in Wyoming during that period (hoof rot at feedgrounds, winter weather mortality), but none specifically along I-80 and none matching the scale of "hundreds" of animals. If such an event occurred and was not reported, it would represent a significant gap in wildlife monitoring and press coverage. It is possible the claim conflates or exaggerates documented events at Wyoming feedgrounds with I-80 proximity.

### 3.4 Official Explanation for Elk Die-Off

No official explanation exists for an I-80 elk die-off because no such event has been documented in available public records. For the documented mortality events that did occur:
- **Hoof rot at Horse Creek Feedground:** Officially attributed to bacterial infection (chronic laminitis), killing "nearly half of the congregated calves"
- **Winter mortality:** Attributed to severe winter conditions and inadequate forage
- **CWD deaths:** Attributed to prion disease, individually fatal over extended periods

## 4. Wikipedia Absence Analysis

### 4.1 I-80 Elk Die-Off — Wikipedia Article

**Confirmed:** No Wikipedia article exists for an "I-80 elk die-off" or any related title. Searches for this topic return only generic elk biology articles. This is consistent with the event being unverified — Wikipedia does not document events that have not been reported in reliable sources.

### 4.2 Freemasonry in Wyoming — Wikipedia Article

**Confirmed:** No Wikipedia article exists for "Freemasonry in Wyoming" (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freemasonry_in_Wyoming returns 404). This is a genuine absence — Wyoming is one of the 50 US states with an active Masonic Grand Lodge, and the absence of a state-specific article represents a gap in Wikipedia's coverage.

### 4.3 Freemasonry in Other Comparable States — Wikipedia Articles

**Tested and confirmed 404 for ALL tested states:**
- "Freemasonry in Montana" → 404 (no article)
- "Freemasonry in Utah" → 404 (no article)
- "Freemasonry in Colorado" → 404 (no article)
- "Freemasonry in Idaho" → 404 (no article)

**This means the absence of a Wyoming article is NOT unique to Wyoming** — it reflects a broader Wikipedia content gap where state-level Freemasonry articles do not exist for most Western states. The main article "Freemasonry in the United States" covers the topic nationally, and the "List of Freemasons" provides biographical entries, but state-specific articles are largely absent for Western states.

**Wikipedia's Freemasonry coverage includes:**
- "Freemasonry in the United States" (general national article — exists, covers colonial era through Civil War, Anti-Masonic Party, Albert Pike, etc.)
- "List of Freemasons" (alphabetized biographical lists, A-D and E-Z)
- "List of Tennessee Freemasons" (the only state-specific list found)
- "Category:Freemasonry in the United States by state" (category page — returned 404, suggesting it may not exist or may be structured differently)

### 4.4 Grand Lodge of Wyoming — Online Presence

**Research findings:**

1. **wyomingfreemasons.org** — DNS resolution failed (ENOTFOUND). Domain does not exist or has expired.
2. **wyomingfreemasonry.org** — DNS resolution failed.
3. **wlofwyoming.org** — DNS resolution failed.
4. **glofy.com** (guess: "Grand Lodge Of Freemasonry, Wyoming") — Returns 109 bytes, unclear content — does not appear to be the Grand Lodge of Wyoming.
5. **wyo.gov** (state of Wyoming official site) — Fetch failed. The state government site does not host Grand Lodge content (as expected — Masonic organizations are private, not state entities).

**Assessment:** The Grand Lodge of Wyoming does not appear to have an active, easily discoverable web presence at any of the obvious domain names. This is unusual for an active Masonic Grand Lodge — most state Grand Lodges maintain websites (e.g., the Grand Lodge of Utah has a website, as do most states). The absence of a Grand Lodge of Wyoming website, combined with the absence of a Wikipedia article, creates a documented information gap about Freemasonry in Wyoming specifically.

**Context:** Freemasonry is documented as active in Wyoming historically. The main "Freemasonry in the United States" Wikipedia article notes that after the American Revolution, "the various Provincial Grand Lodges were dissolved, and the Lodges within each state formed independent Grand Lodges. These new Grand Lodges, in turn, chartered lodges in the expanding western territories and newly admitted states." Wyoming became a state in 1890; Masonic lodges would have been established during the territorial period (1868-1890) alongside the Union Pacific Railroad's construction.

## 7. Research Limitations

- **Web search API was rate-limited** (Gemini 429 RESOURCE_EXHAUSTED) for much of this research, limiting the ability to do broad web searches.
- **DuckDuckGo** was captcha-blocked.
- **Google Search** returned empty JS-rendered pages via web_fetch.
- **Bing Search** returned results but matched on individual words rather than phrases, producing irrelevant results for most queries.
- **PHMSA, NTSB, and some government databases** returned 403/404 errors.
- **Local news archives** (KSL, Standard-Examiner, KUER) returned 404 for guessed URLs — articles may exist but at different URL patterns.
- **Uintah County property records** and **Vernal historical society archives** were not accessible via available tools.
