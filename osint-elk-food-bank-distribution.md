# OSINT Investigation: Elk Carcass Distribution to Rock Springs, WY Food Bank (Early 2023)

## Investigation Date: 2026-06-28
## Investigator: OSINT Subagent
## Classification: OPEN SOURCE INTELLIGENCE REPORT

---

## Executive Summary

This investigation attempted to verify claims by Steven Crawford-Maggard that hundreds of elk carcasses found dead along I-80 in Wyoming in late February/early March 2023 (attributed to "winter kill") were donated to a Rock Springs, Wyoming food bank. **The investigation was unable to find any corroborating evidence** of this claim through open-source research. However, significant regulatory and contextual evidence was uncovered that **directly contradicts** the feasibility of such a donation under Wyoming law and WGFD policy.

## 3. Who Authorized the Donation

### Findings:
- **No evidence of authorization was found** because no evidence of the donation itself was found.
- The **Wyoming Game and Fish Department (WGFD)** is the primary state agency responsible for wildlife management, including carcass disposal.
- WGFD maintains explicit **Carcass Disposal** and **Carcass Disposal Sites** web pages, both focused on CWD (Chronic Wasting Disease) prevention, not food bank donation.
- The WGFD **Roadkill Collection** page (wgfd.wyo.gov/licenses-applications/permits/roadkill-collection) contains two critical regulatory constraints (see Section 7 below).

### Assessment:
**NO EVIDENCE OF AUTHORIZATION FOUND** — No agency authorization for donating elk carcasses to food banks was discovered. The WGFD's own regulations appear to prohibit such donations (see Section 7).

## 4. Food Safety Concerns

### Findings:
- No evidence was found that any USDA or Wyoming Department of Agriculture inspection of elk meat occurred in relation to a food bank donation.
- The WGFD's roadkill collection regulations explicitly state that **roadkill-collected meat cannot be donated to a non-profit organization** — this strongly implies food safety concerns are embedded in the regulatory framework.
- The severe winter conditions documented in the CSD articles (temperatures to -42°F, deep snow, wind) would not necessarily prevent bacterial growth or ensure meat safety, as temperature fluctuations could cause freeze-thaw cycles.
- **No necropsy or toxicology testing** of the dead elk has been documented (per the investigation's background context). Without determining cause of death, distributing meat from these animals would pose unknown food safety risks.
- CWD (Chronic Wasting Disease) is a significant concern in Wyoming elk populations. The WGFD's carcass disposal regulations are specifically designed around CWD transmission prevention. Distributing uninspected elk meat from animals found dead of unknown causes would bypass all CWD safety protocols.

### Assessment:
**SIGNIFICANT SAFETY CONCERNS IDENTIFIED** — Even if the donation occurred, it would have bypassed all standard food safety inspection protocols. The lack of necropsy/toxicology testing, combined with CWD concerns, means the meat would have been unfit for human consumption by any reasonable regulatory standard.

## 7. Wyoming Regulations on Donating Wild Game Meat to Food Banks

### **CRITICAL FINDING**

The WGFD Roadkill Collection page (https://wgfd.wyo.gov/licenses-applications/permits/roadkill-collection) contains two regulations that directly bear on the investigation's central claim:

#### Regulation 1: Interstate Collection Prohibition
> **"Roadkill cannot be collected on Interstate 25, Interstate-80 or Interstate-90"**

This means that if elk died along I-80 (as claimed), **collecting their carcasses under the roadkill permit system would be illegal**. The specific prohibition on I-80 collection makes the claim that "elk carcasses were rounded up along I-80 and donated to a food bank" directly contradictory to WGFD's own regulations.

#### Regulation 2: Donation Prohibition
> **"No, roadkill-collected meat cannot be donated to a non-profit organization."**

This is an explicit prohibition on donating roadkill-collected meat to non-profit organizations — which would include food banks. This regulation directly contradicts the claim that elk carcasses (found dead, i.e., effectively roadkill/winterkill) were donated to a food bank.

#### Additional WGFD Carcass Disposal Regulations
The WGFD maintains separate carcass disposal guidance focused on CWD prevention:
- Whole carcasses can be transported to "a camp, private residence for processing, a taxidermist, a processor, or a CWD sample collection site" — **food banks are not listed as authorized destinations**.
- Approved disposal methods are landfills and incinerators — **not food banks**.

#### Wyoming Statutes
Wyoming Title 23 (Game and Fish) statutes were not fully accessible via curl (likely require JavaScript rendering), but the WGFD regulatory framework as published on their website clearly does not contemplate or authorize donation of found-dead wildlife to food banks.

### Assessment:
**REGULATIONS PROHIBIT THE CLAIMED DONATION** — Wyoming's own regulations through the WGFD explicitly (1) prohibit collecting roadkill on I-80, and (2) prohibit donating roadkill-collected meat to non-profit organizations. These regulations make the claimed donation of I-80 elk carcasses to a Rock Springs food bank **regulatory impossibility** under standard WGFD procedures.

## Conclusions

### Confidence Assessment: MODERATE-HIGH that the claimed elk-to-food-bank donation did NOT occur as described

Based on the available evidence:

1. **No corroborating news coverage** was found in Wyoming's largest newspaper (CSD) despite extensive searching
2. **Wyoming regulations explicitly prohibit** collecting roadkill on I-80 and donating roadkill meat to non-profit organizations
3. **WGFD carcass disposal policies** focus on CWD prevention through landfill/incineration, not food bank donation
4. **The source (Steven Crawford-Maggard)** could not be independently verified
5. **No food safety inspection** documentation was found
6. **The winter of 2022-2023 was severe** — wildlife mortality is well-documented, but the specific claim about I-80 elk die-off and food bank donation is uncorroborated

### Possible Alternative Explanations:
1. **The donation occurred outside official channels** — Local officials may have informally arranged for carcasses to be processed and distributed without following WGFD regulations. This would explain the absence of official documentation.
2. **The claim is inaccurate or exaggerated** — The severity of the winter is real, but the specific detail about food bank donation may be conflated from other events (e.g., the buffalo bounty/Wind River food pantry story documented by WyoFile).
3. **A different food bank or location was involved** — The claim specifies Rock Springs, but the event may have occurred in a different Wyoming community.
4. **The story was reported in a source not accessible** to this investigation (e.g., Rocket-Miner, Casper Star-Tribune, or local TV news that were rate-limited/blocked).

### Recommendations for Further Investigation:
1. **Contact WGFD directly** — Request any records of elk die-off along I-80 in early 2023 and any documentation of carcass disposal methods used
2. **Search the Rocket-Miner archives** — The Rock Springs local newspaper is the most likely source for local coverage but was inaccessible during this investigation
3. **Submit Wyoming public records requests** — For WGFD records related to elk mortality events in Sweetwater County in Feb-March 2023
4. **Verify Steven Crawford-Maggard's identity** — Determine if this individual is a WGFD employee, local official, journalist, or private citizen
5. **Contact Food Bank of the Rockies/Wyoming Food Bank** — Ask directly if they received any game meat donations in early 2023
6. **Check Wyoming Department of Health records** — For any foodborne illness alerts in Sweetwater County in spring 2023
