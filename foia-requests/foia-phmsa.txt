CONTEXT AND BASIS FOR REQUEST

On or about March 2, 2023, a Union Pacific train derailed at the Ogden, Utah rail yard, resulting in approximately 12 rail cars leaving the tracks. Union Pacific publicly stated that no hazardous materials were released, identifying the cargo only as magnesium chloride. This event was listed by VICE/Motherboard among approximately 15 hazmat-related derailments in the period following the East Palestine, Ohio disaster.

In the hours following the Ogden derailment, a blue chemical fog was observed along the I-80 corridor between Evanston, Wyoming and Fort Bridger, Wyoming, with multiple individuals found unconscious in their vehicles. Magnesium chloride, as commonly transported, is not typically associated with the formation of a visible chemical fog or the acute loss of consciousness observed. The nature of the observed fog and its health effects are more consistent with other hazardous materials — such as cyclohexane (a volatile organic compound that can cause central nervous system depression and unconsciousness at sufficient concentrations) or ammonium nitrate (which can release toxic gases and has been associated with catastrophic incidents) — raising questions about whether the full cargo manifest was accurately reported.

PHMSA is the primary federal agency responsible for regulating and investigating hazardous materials transportation incidents. Its records are therefore essential to determining: (a) whether the full hazardous materials manifest of the derailed train was accurately reported; (b) whether hazardous materials were in fact released; (c) whether the observed chemical fog and public health effects in Wyoming can be linked to the Ogden derailment or other UP operations; and (d) whether PHMSA conducted adequate oversight and investigation of this incident.
