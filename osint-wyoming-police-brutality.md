## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report documents documented cases of police brutality, excessive force, and in-custody injuries in Laramie and Cheyenne, Wyoming. The investigation identified multiple civil rights lawsuits, federal appellate cases, and patterns of use-of-force incidents involving the Laramie Police Department, Cheyenne Police Department, Albany County Detention Center, Laramie County Detention Center, and related law enforcement agencies. While several cases involve deaths in custody and serious physical injury, no publicly documented case was found that specifically documents brain injury to an inmate caused by police brutality in these jurisdictions. However, the cases below show a pattern of force resulting in death, fractured jaws, and other severe injuries — making brain injury a plausible outcome in some instances.

## 1. KEY CASES IDENTIFIED

### 1A. CRUZ v. CITY OF LARAMIE — Death in Custody (Hog-Tie Restraint)
- **Citation:** 239 F.3d 1183 (10th Cir. 2001)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals, Tenth Circuit
- **Date:** February 15, 2001
- **Docket Nos.:** 99-8045, 99-8049, 99-8050
- **Source:** OpenJurist (full text retrieved), CourtListener (listing confirmed)

**FACTS:**
On June 10, 1996, Laramie Police Department officers responded to a complaint about a naked man, Thomas C. Cruz, running around acting erratically. Four Laramie police officers — Bonnie Noel, Richard D. Michel, Troy Jensen, and Ben Fritzen — responded. Cruz was clearly experiencing diminished capacity (later confirmed to have large amounts of cocaine in his system). Officers wrestled him to the ground, handcuffed him face down, and Officer Fritzen applied a "hog-tie" restraint (binding ankles to wrists behind the back with ≤12 inches separation). Shortly after, Cruz's face blanched, the restraint was removed, and CPR was begun. Cruz was pronounced dead on arrival at the hospital.

**LEGAL ISSUES:**
- 42 U.S.C. § 1983 excessive force claim (Fourth Amendment)
- Wyoming Governmental Claims Act state negligence claim
- Qualified immunity defense

**HOLDING:**
- The 10th Circuit held that the Fourth Amendment protects individuals from hog-tie restraints when diminished capacity is apparent.
- Officers' diminished capacity awareness was apparent from the moment they arrived.
- However, the court reversed denial of qualified immunity on federal claims because the law was not "clearly established" at the time of the 1996 incident.
- State law immunity was denied because Wyoming requires reasonableness (not "clearly established" law), and officers acted unreasonably.
- The City of Laramie faced failure-to-train liability.

**RELEVANCE TO BRAIN INJURY:**
While this case involved death by positional asphyxia (not brain injury per se), the hog-tie restraint technique and the failure to monitor a person in distress could easily result in brain injury from oxygen deprivation. The case established important precedent for restraint-related custody deaths in Wyoming.

### 1B. CARABAJAL v. CITY OF CHEYENNE, WY — Excessive Force
- **Citation:** 847 F.3d 1203 (10th Cir. 2017)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals, Tenth Circuit
- **Date:** February 6, 2017
- **Docket No.:** 15-8139
- **Source:** CourtListener (listing and metadata confirmed)

**FACTS:**
Civil rights lawsuit against the City of Cheyenne and Officers Josh Thornton and Michael Sutton. The case involved excessive force claims. The 10th Circuit addressed qualified immunity standards for the excessive force claims and a negligent hiring claim.

**KEY DETAILS:**
- The case is frequently cited in subsequent 10th Circuit excessive force cases (Knopf v. Williams, 884 F.3d 939 (10th Cir. 2018), and others).
- The court addressed the standard for granting qualified immunity in excessive force cases.
- A concurrence opinion addressed Mr. Carabajal's excessive force claims specifically.

**RELEVANCE:**
This is a direct excessive force case against Cheyenne police officers. The specific injuries are not fully detailed in the available metadata, but the case establishes that Cheyenne PD has faced federal civil rights litigation for use of force.

### 1E. CLAPPIER v. FLYNN (Laramie County Sheriff) — Jail Assault, Fractured Jaw
- **Citation:** 605 F.2d 519 (10th Cir. 1979)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals, Tenth Circuit
- **Date:** September 4, 1979
- **Docket No.:** 78-1109
- **Source:** OpenJurist (full text retrieved)

**FACTS:**
Craig Charles Clappier was arrested by Cheyenne city police and booked into the Laramie County Jail (operated by Sheriff Dennis Flynn). Over a period of approximately 36 hours, Clappier was repeatedly beaten, sexually assaulted, and threatened by other inmates. He suffered a fractured jaw, severe bruising, and was held at Fitzsimmons Army Medical Center for over four weeks with his jaw wired shut.

**LEGAL ISSUES:**
- 42 U.S.C. § 1983 — cruel and unusual punishment (Eighth Amendment)
- Negligent supervision of jail by Laramie County Sheriff
- Wyoming Constitution: "No person arrested and confined in jail shall be treated with unnecessary rigor"

**HOLDING:**
- Jury awarded $24,500 in damages against the Sheriff of Laramie County.
- The court found the sheriff failed to adequately supervise and inspect the jail.
- Deputies made only perfunctory "head counts" and never entered the cell block area.
- The case established that sheriffs have a duty to protect inmates from known dangers.

**RELEVANCE TO BRAIN INJURY:**
While the documented injury was a fractured jaw, the severity of the beating (40-50 strikes to the face, head, and groin) makes it plausible that head/brain injury occurred. The case demonstrates a pattern of inadequate supervision at Laramie County Detention Center that could allow serious injuries (including brain injuries) to go undetected.

### 1F. HOPKINS v. COUNTY OF LARAMIE — Excessive Force (Police Shooting)
- **Citation:** 730 F.2d 603 (10th Cir. 1984)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals, Tenth Circuit
- **Date:** March 23, 1984
- **Docket No.:** 82-2208
- **Source:** OpenJurist (full text retrieved)

**FACTS:**
A § 1983 action against Laramie County, Wyoming, and four police officers. The case involved a police pursuit of Raymond and Martha Hopkins from a Cheyenne motel, during which officers fired shots at the vehicle. The pursuit ended with the Hopkins' car colliding with a police car, additional shots fired, Mrs. Hopkins shot (apparently by her husband), and Mr. Hopkins apparently committing suicide. The suit alleged excessive force and inadequate training.

**HOLDING:**
Jury found defendants not negligent. Affirmed on appeal.

**RELEVANCE:**
Demonstrates a pattern of use of lethal force by Laramie County law enforcement, though the jury sided with defendants.

## CONCLUSION

Wyoming law enforcement agencies in Laramie and Cheyenne have a documented history of excessive force and in-custody injury cases spanning from 1975 to 2025. The most relevant lead for brain injury is **Dunsmore v. State (2007)**, which references a subdural hematoma and Cheyenne police. **Cruz v. City of Laramie (2001)** documents a death from hog-tie restraint by Laramie PD — a technique that can cause brain injury from oxygen deprivation. **Clappier v. Flynn (1979)** shows Laramie County Jail's failure to supervise resulted in severe beatings. **Carabajal v. City of Cheyenne (2017)** confirms Cheyenne PD faced federal excessive force litigation. The 2025 Hemmer case demonstrates ongoing Wyoming law enforcement liability issues.

For a specific brain injury case involving an inmate in Laramie or Cheyenne detention facilities, the most promising avenues are: (1) obtaining the full text of Dunsmore v. State, (2) PACER searches for sealed or unpublished federal cases in the District of Wyoming, (3) Wyoming public records requests for internal affairs complaints, and (4) contacting ACLU Wyoming and Wyoming Protection & Advocacy for unpublished complaint data.
