## 1. Mormon/LDS Influence in Uinta County

### Demographics
- **Uinta County: 49.5% LDS** (2020 ARDA data) — the dominant religious institution
- Other: Evangelical 7.7%, Mainline Protestant 1.4%, Catholic 2.0%, None 37.8%
- County seat: Evanston (pop. 11,747)
- Total county pop: 20,450

### LDS/UP Historical Connection
- **Utah Central Railroad** (1869-1870): Built by Mormons under Brigham Young to connect Salt Lake City to the transcontinental railroad at Ogden. UP had deliberately bypassed Salt Lake City, which Young considered "an insult to the people of this city."
- UP couldn't pay Mormon workers for grading the line through Utah — settled the debt by transferring rails, supplies, and rolling stock to the Mormons. This gave UP's physical infrastructure to the LDS Church.
- Brigham Young hammered a spike engraved "Holiness to the Lord" at the Utah Central's completion ceremony.
- The Utah Central later became part of Union Pacific and is now the Salt Lake Subdivision.

### Fort Bridger LDS Seizure
- Fort Bridger (founded 1842 by Jim Bridger) was seized by Mormon militia in 1853 on Brigham Young's orders
- Uinta County was created December 1, 1869, with temporary seat at Fort Bridger
- Fort Bridger State Historic Site remains a protected area

### Mormon Settlement Pattern
- Mormon pioneers settled Piedmont (1867) specifically as a UP railroad town
- Moses Byrne and Charles Guild founded Piedmont for UP railroad construction
- Charcoal kilns built 1877 (3 still on National Register of Historic Places)
- UP bypassed Piedmont in 1910 via Aspen tunnel → town died
- Pattern: LDS builds town for UP → UP abandons town when convenient

## 3. Dale Eaton Case (Lil' Miss Murder)

### Summary

## 4. Almy, Wyoming — Coal Mining Ghost Town

### Summary
- Coal mining camp near Evanston, Uinta County
- Named for James T. Almy, clerk for Rocky Mountain Coal Company
- 4,000 residents at peak, strung along Bear River for 5 miles
- **Three major mining disasters:**
  - March 4, 1881: Central Pacific Mine No. 3 explosion — 38 killed (35 Chinese, 3 white)
  - 1886: Second explosion — 13 killed
  - 1895: Red Canyon explosion — 62 killed (third worst mining disaster in Wyoming history)
- Mines shut down in 1940s — deemed too dangerous
- Chinese workers were primary labor force

### Significance
- Another Uinta County ghost town created by extractive industry (coal for UP locomotives)
- Chinese labor exploited, killed in explosions, then erased from history
- Connects to Piedmont pattern: extractive industry builds town → abandonment → ghost town
- Fire raged in Central Pacific Mine for 5 years before the 1881 explosion — institutional negligence
